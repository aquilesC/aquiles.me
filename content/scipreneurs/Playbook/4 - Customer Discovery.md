The most important experiment happens outside the lab.  
  
As scientists, we are masters of the peer-review process. We know how to defend a thesis and present at a conference.  
But when it comes to spinning out a company, our biggest strength quickly becomes our biggest liability.  
  
We can call it the "Seminar Trap."  
  
We are so good at talking about our selves and our work that we forget the relevance of just listening with intent.  
  
Customer Discovery isn't a sales pitch. It’s a truth-finding mission.  
  
To build a robust business case, you have to stop being the Professor and start being the Psychologist. Here are the 3 rules for "talking to humans" about your science:  
  
1️⃣ Don't mention your solution: If you tell them what you’ve built, they will lie to you to be nice. Instead, ask them about the last time they faced the problem you’re trying to solve.  
  
2️⃣ Focus on the "Workflow," not the "Tech": Don't ask if they like your enzyme. Ask how many hours a week they spend on filtration and what happens to their budget if that process fails.  
  
3️⃣ Listen for the "Ouch": You aren't looking for "interest." You are looking for a pain point so significant that they’ve already tried (and failed) to find a workaround.  
  
The goal of these conversations isn't to get a "yes." It's to find the unvarnished truth of the market before you spend the next three years building something nobody actually wants to buy.  
  
Customer discovery is often mistaken with sales. While a transaction can happen at an early stage, the scope is to learn: who are your customers? Do they see the same problems you have identified or have they moved on? Who pays, who decides, who uses?  
  
The earlier you start with the discovery process, the easier it’ll be to avoid starting a path that leads nowhere.
